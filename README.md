# AAD_Fagprojekt

_Decoding auditory attention in speech perception from EEG data_

The purpose is to investigate how hearing aids could be improved through the use of real time EEG signals. By understanding where the attention is directed, amplifying the signal in question and attenuating the surrounding noise could significantly improve life quality for both patients suffering from hearing loss, but also a vast group of users in need of improved sound isolation for various reasons.

The struggle of modern hearing aids is their inability to detect the direction of the user’s attention and understand who the user is listening to, which is something studies show the human brain is good at. Patients suffering from hearing loss are struggling with their hearing aid devices in crowded settings with multiple sources of sound, as all sounds are being amplified, making it noisy and exhaustive and lowering their life quality. Previously, the effect of audio stimuli on EEG signals has been measured in a trial based setting with repetitive exposures of the same stimuli in order to average out the random noise and enhance the effect of the presented stimuli. However, this trial based approach differs vastly from how speech perception works in real life settings making the results less representational for how speech perception can be decoded from EEG data in real life settings. Therefore, researchers have recently moved from a trial based approach to looking at speech signals over continuous time and applying a temporal response function (TRF) that maps from the sound input to the measured EEG signals.

Based on the approach of continuous input it is investigated how real-time EEG signals obtained through the user’s ears could be used to decode their attention, and how this could improve the quality of hearing aid devices. Furthermore, it is investigated how to avoid vivid fluctuations of amplified sounds for a smooth user experience.
